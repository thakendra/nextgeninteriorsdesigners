The Mustang region of Nepal is one of the world's most extraordinary landscapes — a high-altitude desert kingdom of ancient monasteries, craggy cliffs, and a distinct Tibetan-influenced culture. When the team behind D-Moksha Mustang Thakali approached NextGen Interiors, their brief was simple but powerful: bring Mustang to Kathmandu. What followed was one of our most immersive and culturally rich restaurant design projects to date.

Project Overview

D-Moksha Mustang Thakali is a premium Thakali dining restaurant located in Kathmandu. Thakali cuisine — hearty, nourishing, and deeply rooted in the Mustang and Thak Khola valley traditions — deserved a home that matched its authenticity. The client wanted a space that was not a caricature of culture, but a genuinely respectful and beautifully executed homage to the Mustangi way of life.

NextGen Interiors was entrusted with the complete interior design — from space planning and material selection to furniture, lighting, and decorative elements. The project covered the main dining area, private dining room, reception entrance, and kitchen service corridor.

Design Concept — The Mustangi Spirit

Every great restaurant interior begins with a single powerful idea. For D-Moksha, our concept was "Lo Manthang at Dusk" — evoking the warm ochre light that bathes Mustang's ancient capital as the sun descends behind the Himalayas. This guided every decision from wall colour to fixture choice.

◈
Design Concept
"Lo Manthang at Dusk" — the warm ochre light of Mustang's ancient capital at sunset. Every material, colour, and texture in D-Moksha was chosen to evoke this singular, unforgettable image.

The palette draws from the natural landscape of Upper Mustang: terracotta and burnt sienna walls, natural stone flooring reminiscent of the Kali Gandaki riverbed, raw timber beams evoking the traditional Mustangi home architecture, and hand-woven textiles in deep reds and indigos — colours worn by the region's Loba people for centuries.

"We didn't want guests to feel they were looking at Mustang through a window — we wanted them to feel they had arrived there. Every element had to earn its place."

— NextGen Interiors Design Team, on D-Moksha

Entrance & First Impressions

The entrance to D-Moksha sets the tone before a guest even sits down. A carved timber archway — reminiscent of Mustangi monastery doorways — frames the entry. Prayer flag motifs are subtly etched into the stone threshold, and a hand-painted Thangka-inspired mural panel welcomes guests on the left wall.

The reception counter is crafted from reclaimed pine timber with hand-forged iron fixtures. A single brass butter lamp — oversized and dramatic — hangs above the host stand, casting a warm golden glow that immediately signals the atmosphere within.

Dining Hall Design

The main dining hall accommodates up to 60 guests across a mix of banquet-style community tables and intimate booth seating. Community dining is central to Thakali culture, and our layout honours this — long shared tables dominate the central floor, encouraging the kind of convivial atmosphere that Thakali meals have always embodied.

Seating & Layout

Booth seating lines the perimeter walls, upholstered in hand-loomed Dhaka fabric — a textile tradition of Nepal — in deep crimson and indigo. The central community tables are hewn from solid pine, left in a natural lightly-oiled finish. Chair backs are woven in a geometric pattern inspired by traditional Mustangi rug designs.

Ceiling Design

The ceiling is perhaps the most dramatic element of the dining hall. Exposed dark-stained timber beams run the length of the space, from which hang clusters of hand-thrown clay pots — traditional Mustangi vessels — converted into pendant light fixtures. The effect is organic, warm, and utterly unique.

Materials & Craftsmanship

What makes D-Moksha extraordinary is not any single element — it is the cumulative effect of dozens of carefully chosen materials, all sourced and crafted with intention. NextGen Interiors worked with local artisans from across Nepal to bring the vision to life.

  • Stone flooring — Grey slate sourced from the Kali Gandaki river valley, laid in an irregular pattern that mimics the natural riverbed
  • Wall finishes — Hand-applied clay plaster in terracotta tones, textured with traditional tools to create an organic, uneven surface
  • Timber — Reclaimed pine and deodar cedar beams, salvaged and treated — chosen for their age and character
  • Textiles — Hand-loomed Dhaka fabric from Palpa and Tansen for upholstery; imported Tibetan wool rugs for floor accents
  • Metalwork — Hand-forged iron brackets, hinges, and fixtures made by blacksmiths in Patan's metalworking district
  • Ceramics — Traditional Mustangi clay pots and bowls, sourced directly from Upper Mustang village potters
  • Artwork — Original Thangka-inspired murals hand-painted by a Newar artist commissioned for the project

Lighting Design

Lighting in D-Moksha was designed to shift through the day — bright and warm at lunch, progressively more amber and intimate as evening falls. All lighting is on a smart dimmer system, allowing the restaurant to set different moods for breakfast service, lunch dining, and evening hospitality.

◎
Lighting Philosophy
Three lighting layers — ambient (clay pot pendants), task (recessed warm LED over tables), and accent (butter lamp replicas along walls) — create depth and atmosphere at every hour of the day.

The signature feature is the brass butter lamp wall installation — a row of 24 oversized brass lamp replicas mounted at eye level along the longest wall of the dining hall. Backlit with warm amber LEDs, they cast pools of golden light that ripple across the terracotta plaster, evoking the atmosphere of a Mustangi gompa at prayer time.

Private Dining & Lounge Area

Beyond the main hall, D-Moksha features a private dining room for up to 16 guests — ideal for family celebrations, corporate lunches, and private events. The design here is more intimate and opulent: deeper colours, richer textiles, and a striking hand-carved wooden screen that divides the space from the main hall while allowing filtered light and sound to pass through.

A small waiting lounge near the entrance features low seating inspired by traditional Mustangi sitting platforms, cushioned in hand-embroidered textile covers. A carved wooden display shelf houses a curated collection of Mustangi artefacts — copper water jugs, yak wool blankets, and stone carvings — lending the space an authenticity that no catalogue item could match.

The Finished Space

The completed D-Moksha Mustang Thakali restaurant is a testament to what happens when deep cultural respect meets skilled interior design. Guests who have never visited Mustang feel transported there. Those who know the region recognise the authenticity in every detail. And the food — the exceptional Thakali cuisine — is served in a setting worthy of its heritage.

Since opening, D-Moksha has received widespread praise for its interior — featured in local media as one of Kathmandu's most distinctive dining environments. The restaurant has become a destination in itself, with guests arriving as much for the atmosphere as for the food.

"NextGen Interiors understood Mustang. They didn't design a restaurant — they designed a feeling. Every guest who walks in asks who designed this space. That says everything."

— Client, D-Moksha Mustang Thakali
